## Review of Module's Success
I succeded in creating and assembling the PCB, however, I failed to account for all the necessary pins and connections.
I ended up needing to switch to a daughterboard and breakout board in order to actually implement my required
functionality. Once there however I managed to actuate the motor using the motor driver and successfully code my PIC.

## Microcontroller/Module Startup Tip List
  * Add connectors to every pin on your microcontroller
  * add whatever you think is an unnecessary amount of test points then add 10 more
  * organize your footprints for a clean layout in sections
  * use solderwick instead of trying to do a perfect initial solder job
  
## Lessons Learned
1) Prioritize a good schematic as its the core to the project.
2) Make sure you fully understand each component you select rather than blindly building a board.
3) Work closer with your teammates to ensure your projects mesh well together.
4) Test your board often in small increments instead of one large check at the end.
5) Take the time to think out how the board is actually going to work not just how you think it should work.
6) If you think a part is broken it is.
7) Order as many extra parts as you can afford. (I blew up loads of parts)
8) Make sure every pin to every part has an extra way to connect just in case you mess up so you can avoid cutting traces.
9) Make sure you update your github often even if its not correct. (Do what I say not what I do)
10) Actually ensure your board is wired properly before troubleshooting the code itself.


## Recommendations for future students
1) Plan out your team members roles and goals not just who does what.
2) Plan to spend 3 times longer on your schematic than you'd expect.
3) Make sure to ask TA's and Professors for assistance earlier rather than later.
4) Keep your basis simple a cool large project is nice until the deadline creeps up.
5) Focus on small completion parts rather than the whole system at once.
